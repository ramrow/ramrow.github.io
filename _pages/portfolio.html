Alpha Lab

Document Overview

The document is a comprehensive laboratory report detailing experiments and mathematical analyses of fundamental electrical networks, specifically focusing on RC (Resistor-Capacitor) and RLC (Resistor-Inductor-Capacitor) circuits[cite: 1]. The primary objective of the document is to validate theoretical circuit behaviors by triangulating data from three sources: hand-written analytical equations, LTspice software simulations, and physical hardware measurements captured using an ADALM1000 Oscilloscope[cite: 1].

Part A & B: RC Circuits and Time Constants

In the first two sections (Part A and Part B), the report successfully demonstrates the principles of RC time constants (τ = RC)[cite: 1].

  • The author proves that changing the resistor and capacitor values directly alters the time it takes for the capacitor's voltage to reach 63.2% of its final value during a step input[cite: 1].
  • The document compares the theoretical time constants to the measured oscilloscope data, achieving results well within acceptable error margins (e.g., 5.6% and 3.37%)[cite: 1].
  • Furthermore, the author successfully utilizes Thevenin's theorem to simplify a multi-resistor network into an equivalent circuit, mathematically predicting and physically verifying that the capacitor's steady-state voltage approaches 1/3 V[cite: 1].

Part C: RLC Circuit Dynamics

The most extensive accomplishment of the report (Part C) is its deep exploration of a series RLC circuit[cite: 1].

  • The author successfully proves how manipulating a single resistance value shifts the system's dynamic response between overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped states[cite: 1].
  • Through rigorous time-domain calculations and s-domain (Laplace transform) analysis, the author derives the attenuation constant (α) and resonant frequency (ω0) to establish the required resistance thresholds for each damping condition[cite: 1].
  • The report pairs these complex mathematical models with accurate LTspice simulations and physical oscilloscope traces, successfully validating the predicted oscillatory and exponential decay behaviors of both the capacitor's voltage and the inductor's current[cite: 1].

Part D: Timing and Oscillator Configurations

Finally, the document concludes (Part D) by comparing different timing and oscillator configurations, including the basic RC Timer, the 555 Timer IC, and the RC Phase Shift Oscillator[cite: 1].

  • The author successfully simulates an RC Phase Shift Oscillator, proving its ability to reach a steady state and generate a continuous sinusoidal output frequency of approximately 31.5 kHz[cite: 1].
  • The report highlights the practical engineering applications of such an oscillator, noting its utility in audio synthesizers, testing circuits, and GPS modules due to its reliable frequency generation[cite: 1].

Omega Lab

The Omega Lab project represents a complete, dual-powered AM radio receiver built from the ground up. The system architecture includes a custom diode-based power selection circuit designed to seamlessly switch between solar and wall power. The signal processing pipeline features a second-order LC bandpass filter tuned to a local 810 kHz station, followed by an RC envelope detector for AM demodulation. Finally, the audio signal is boosted through a two-stage amplification process, utilizing a non-inverting operational amplifier for voltage gain and a highly efficient Class B push-pull transistor setup to provide the necessary current to drive an 8-ohm speaker.

Fall 2024 Intro to Engineering Design

Mini Project: Line Follower

Co-authors: Merak Dec, Curtis Sung

The Line Follower mini project focused on designing, building, and testing an autonomous vehicle capable of detecting and following a path. The project combined mechanical design, sensor integration, circuit implementation, and iterative testing to tune the robot's response to line position changes. The final system used feedback from line-detection sensors to guide motor control decisions, demonstrating a complete engineering design cycle from requirements and prototyping through performance evaluation.
