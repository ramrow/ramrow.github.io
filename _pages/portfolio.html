Alpha Lab

Document Overview

The document is a comprehensive laboratory report detailing experiments and mathematical analyses of fundamental electrical networks, specifically focusing on RC (Resistor-Capacitor) and RLC (Resistor-Inductor-Capacitor) circuits. The primary objective of the document is to validate theoretical circuit behaviors by triangulating data from three sources: hand-written analytical equations, LTspice software simulations, and physical hardware measurements captured using an ADALM1000 Oscilloscope.

Part A & B: RC Circuits and Time Constants

In the first two sections (Part A and Part B), the report successfully demonstrates the principles of RC time constants (τ = RC).

  • We proved that changing the resistor and capacitor values directly alters the time it takes for the capacitor's voltage to reach 63.2% of its final value during a step input.
  • The document compares the theoretical time constants to the measured oscilloscope data, achieving results well within acceptable error margins (e.g., 5.6% and 3.37%).
  • Furthermore, we successfully utilizes Thevenin's theorem to simplify a multi-resistor network into an equivalent circuit, mathematically predicting and physically verifying that the capacitor's steady-state voltage approaches 1/3 V.

Part C: RLC Circuit Dynamics

The most extensive accomplishment of the report (Part C) is its deep exploration of a series RLC circuit.

  • We successfully proved how manipulating a single resistance value shifts the system's dynamic response between overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped states.
  • Through rigorous time-domain calculations and s-domain (Laplace transform) analysis, the team derives the attenuation constant (α) and resonant frequency (ω0) to establish the required resistance thresholds for each damping condition.
  • The report pairs these complex mathematical models with accurate LTspice simulations and physical oscilloscope traces, successfully validating the predicted oscillatory and exponential decay behaviors of both the capacitor's voltage and the inductor's current.

Part D: Timing and Oscillator Configurations

Finally, the document concludes (Part D) by comparing different timing and oscillator configurations, including the basic RC Timer, the 555 Timer IC, and the RC Phase Shift Oscillator.

  • The team successfully simulates an RC Phase Shift Oscillator, proving its ability to reach a steady state and generate a continuous sinusoidal output frequency of approximately 31.5 kHz.
  • The report highlights the practical engineering applications of such an oscillator, noting its utility in audio synthesizers, testing circuits, and GPS modules due to its reliable frequency generation.

Omega Lab

The Omega Lab project represents a complete, dual-powered AM radio receiver built from the ground up. The system architecture includes a custom diode-based power selection circuit designed to seamlessly switch between solar and wall power. The signal processing pipeline features a second-order LC bandpass filter tuned to a local 810 kHz station, followed by an RC envelope detector for AM demodulation. Finally, the audio signal is boosted through a two-stage amplification process, utilizing a non-inverting operational amplifier for voltage gain and a highly efficient Class B push-pull transistor setup to provide the necessary current to drive an 8-ohm speaker.

Power Management and Source Selection

A unique feature of this design is its dual-power capability, intending to use a solar panel as a primary source and wall power as a backup. To manage this, the team designed a voltage comparator circuit using two diodes connected in parallel with the respective power supplies.

  • The system operates by relying on the forward and reverse bias properties of diodes.
  • When the wall voltage is greater than the solar voltage, the wall-side diode becomes forward-biased (allowing current to pass) while the solar-side diode becomes reverse-biased (blocking current).
  • Conversely, if the solar voltage exceeds the wall voltage, the solar diode allows current to flow while the wall diode blocks it.
  • If both voltages are equal, both diodes are forward-biased, allowing current from both sources.

Signal Reception and Tuning (Second-Order LC Filter)

To pick up local AM radio broadcasts, the team attempted to use a long wire antenna. Because air transmits many different electromagnetic frequencies simultaneously, we engineered a second-order LC (Inductor-Capacitor) bandpass filter to tune into a specific station.

  • We targeted the frequency 810 kHz to pick up the local WGY radio station based in Schenectady, New York.
  • The transfer function and resonant center frequency for our bandpass filter were calculated using the formal equations: $$H(s) = \frac{\frac{s}{RC}}{s^2 + \frac{1}{RC}s + \frac{1}{LC}}$$ $$f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{LC}}$$
  • Ideally, this circuit required a variable tuning capacitor of 19.3nF paired with a 2μH inductor. Because a variable capacitor was unavailable, we used a fixed 12nF capacitor, which shifted our actual center frequency to approximately 1.03 MHz.

Demodulation (First-Order RC Filter)

Once the specific high-frequency AM signal is isolated, the audio information must be extracted from the high-frequency carrier wave.

  • The signal is first rectified using a low-forward-voltage germanium diode, effectively cutting off the negative half of the AC signal.
  • Next, the signal passes through a first-order RC (Resistor-Capacitor) low-pass filter, acting as an envelope detector.
  • Because AM stations broadcast audio up to 5 kHz, the team calculated component values (a 330-ohm resistor and a 0.1μF capacitor) to create a cutoff frequency of 4823 Hz. This successfully rolls off the 810 kHz carrier frequency at a rate of -20 dB/decade, leaving only the audible message behind.

Amplification Stage

The extracted audio signal is too weak to directly drive a physical speaker, requiring a two-stage amplification process.

  • Voltage Amplification: The team used a non-inverting operational amplifier with a 47k-ohm feedback resistor and a 1k-ohm ground resistor to provide significant voltage gain. To prevent the audio signal from clipping, we implemented a voltage divider to DC-bias the signal to 4.5V (exactly half of our 9V supply rail). AC coupling capacitors (22μF and 4.7μF) were also added to remove unwanted DC interference.
  • Current Amplification: Because an op-amp cannot provide the physical current required to move a speaker cone, the team cascaded the op-amp into a Class B push-pull amplifier. This configuration utilizes paired NPN and PNP transistors. Each transistor only conducts for half of the input wave cycle, resulting in high power efficiency and low heat generation.

Challenges, Limitations, and Outcomes

While the mathematical models and software simulations (via LTspice) were highly successful, the physical hardware implementation faced a few engineering realities.

  • The primary failure point was the antenna. A standard AM radio wave is massive, meaning a proper quarter-wavelength wire antenna would need to be roughly 370 meters long. Our 3-meter wire, operated inside a concrete and metal building, completely failed to pick up the WGY signal.
  • To prove the circuit actually worked, the team bypassed the antenna and injected test sinusoidal waves directly into the amplifier stage, successfully producing an amplified audio tone through the 1W, 8-ohm speaker.
  • We also successfully proved the radio receiver logic in simulation by mathematically multiplying a 1 kHz audio signal with an 810 kHz carrier signal and running it through our virtual filters.
  • In our conclusion, we noted that future iterations could be improved by using a ferrite rod antenna to concentrate magnetic fields, and by upgrading the Class B current amplifier to a Class AB amplifier (using diodes or a Vbe multiplier) to eliminate crossover distortion at the zero-crossing point.

Fall 2024 Intro to Engineering Design

Mini Project: Line Follower

Co-authors: Merak Dec, Curtis Sung

The Line Follower mini project focused on designing, building, and testing an autonomous vehicle capable of detecting and following a path. The project combined mechanical design, sensor integration, circuit implementation, and iterative testing to tune the robot's response to line position changes. The final system used feedback from line-detection sensors to guide motor control decisions, demonstrating a complete engineering design cycle from requirements and prototyping through performance evaluation.

Summary of the project

We set out to design and build a low-cost, reliable line-following robot that could track a 3/4" black line on white background, be simple to assemble (≤10 minutes for an amateur), use inexpensive parts (≤\$30), and serve as an educational platform.

Concept development and benchmarking

We researched existing kits and compared sensing and motor options (photoresistors, IR sensors, cameras; DIY H‑bridge vs L298N motor driver). We created a concept selection matrix with criteria like cost, implementation difficulty, efficiency, creativity, and safety, and used it to choose components that balanced affordability and ease of use.

We decided on an Arduino microcontroller for control, an L298N motor driver for the motor unit, and two ground-facing IR sensors for sensing. The design prioritized simplicity and educational accessibility, with the Arduino and breadboard mounted on the chassis, motors driven by the L298N, and a single 9V battery powering the system.

Implementation Details

We placed the two IR sensors 13 cm apart, mounted the Arduino on the breadboard, positioned the L298N at the rear, and connected motors and battery to the driver. The IR sensors’ result LED signaled white (LED on) vs black (LED off). Arduino code read sensor states and issued motor commands (forward, left, right, or stop/black) via the L298N using PWM for speed control and H‑bridge logic for direction.

We sketched and assembled an initial iteration with slanted IR sensors. Early trials showed poor ground detection, causing the robot to veer off track. We iterated by repositioning the IR sensors to point straight down and lower to the ground. This placement change substantially improved line detection and overall performance.

Testing Strategy

We tested progressively: individual components (IR sensors, power/motors), then IR sensors with code, and finally the fully assembled robot. Sensor tests used white paper and three black samples (printed, tape, marker). Motor tests verified each motor responded correctly to motor driver inputs. Code testing confirmed correct terminal output for all four sensor-state combinations.

Results

The robot achieved consistent success on straight lines, curved turns, 90° turns, squiggles, and a hexagon when turn radii were ≥13 cm. Success rates were 100% on those tracks. The infinity-symbol course, with tighter radii and an intersection, produced inconsistent results for repeated trials. Completion time was not measured; accuracy was prioritized over speed.

We learned the importance of sensor placement for reliable detection, the value of iterative testing from components to full system, and that design trade-offs (cost vs performance vs complexity) matter for educational builds. Non-technical lessons included better team communication practices and starting earlier to allow more testing and refinement.

We delivered a low-cost, easy-to-assemble, educational line-follower that met the core objectives for accuracy and simplicity. The project highlighted practical constraints (sensor range, tight-turn handling) and provided a platform that students can rebuild, modify, and learn from.

Large Project: Assisted Wheelchair Transfer System

Co-authors: Ben Driscoll, David Rowe, Matt Hoover, Miles Lafferty

Our team, Iron Volt Mobility, set out to design an assisted wheelchair transfer system for a kindergarten-aged student with an ataxic gait pattern. The goal was to foster greater independence by enabling him to transition seamlessly from a seated position in his wheelchair to a standing position in his Crocodile Gait Trainer without relying on a teacher or assistant. We prioritized safety, reliability, and the utilization of his existing upper body strength to create an accessible environment that would allow him to confidently join a standard class pathway.

During our system concept development, we brainstormed and evaluated several alternatives, including a scissor lift mechanism, ratchet rails, and a drop-seat design. Ultimately, we moved forward with a modular folding table design combined with an automatic braking system, as it provided the best balance of robust support, simplicity, and cost-effectiveness. To efficiently execute the project, we divided the design into five key sub-assemblies: the push platform, the walker-to-wheelchair attachment, the dual-axis torque hinge, the brake clamp, and the brake control.

I personally led the engineering for the Brake Control subsystem, which was crucial for ensuring neither the wheelchair nor the walker moved while the user was standing. I designed an electric braking circuit utilizing an Arduino microprocessor, an L298N DC motor driver, a 15-volt battery, and two 12-volt motors. I wrote the Arduino code to activate the motors via a push button mounted on the armrest, and integrated a red LED to visually signal to the user when the brakes were safely engaged. While our prototype successfully met the performance specification of engaging the brakes in under 5 seconds—averaging 3.9 seconds—we discovered during integration testing that the 12-volt motors only produced 6 Kg/cm of torque, which was insufficient to fully lock the wheels against significant force without upgrading to servo or strong stepper motors.

To manage our engineering process and timing, we utilized a comprehensive Gantt chart to track task completion across our three major milestones: concept introduction, prototyping/demos, and final presentations. We established our team hierarchy early on and maintained open communication using platforms like Cisco WebEx Teams and iMessage. Although we faced challenges like procrastination and the late integration of our individual subsystems, we successfully navigated these hurdles to deliver a cohesive proof-of-concept. This project not only sharpened my technical skills in circuitry and microcontroller programming but also reinforced the importance of early task allocation and continuous testing in the engineering design cycle.

Fall 2025 Multidisciplinary Capstone: Aircraft Robotic Assembly

Project client: Boeing

Co-authors: Darwin Capani, Talon Carsto, Anderson Corriero, Jeremia Silaban, Jay Tang, Brody Waters

The Aircraft Robotic Assembly capstone focused on improving a robotic assistant for aircraft cable bundle transport and installation. The long-term goal is to reduce the physical strain placed on technicians during cable routing while keeping the robot safe, interpretable, and reliable in a simulated aircraft manufacturing environment.

System Goals and Client Needs

Building on earlier teams' ROS 2 foundation, our Fall 2025 team refined autonomous behavior, operator feedback, cable handling, and safety. The system was designed to support both tele-operation and autonomous operation, with the robot eventually navigating to a cable, picking it up, and positioning it for technician installation.

  • Improve autonomous navigation and cable detection for a more realistic Boeing-style assembly workflow.
  • Upgrade the robot's computing platform while staying within power and compatibility constraints.
  • Provide clearer robot-state feedback so operators and developers can quickly understand system behavior.
  • Improve cable gripping reliability across multiple cable diameters.
  • Add safety features that address emergency shutdown and LiDAR blind spots.

Software, Perception, and Autonomy

A major software contribution was the cable detection and localization pipeline. The system integrates an Intel RealSense RGB-D camera with ROS 2, detects cable pixels in the RGB image, samples aligned depth at those pixels, and converts the result into camera-frame XYZ coordinates. The node publishes an overlay image and 3D point data so downstream autonomy modules can use cable location information without tightly coupling to the perception code.

The team also worked on autonomous navigation improvements for ramp handling and drift correction. A compass-based orientation approach was selected to help detect heading deviations and create a path toward more reliable traversal of inclined surfaces.

Hardware and Human-Robot Interaction

The robot's computation hardware was upgraded from a Raspberry Pi 4 to a Raspberry Pi 5 to provide stronger processing capability while remaining compatible with the robot's existing software stack. We also added a visual feedback subsystem using addressable LEDs to communicate operating states such as autonomous mode, tele-operation, emergency stop, and debugging conditions.

The gripper was redesigned around a fractal-vise-inspired geometry to increase contact area and improve cable retention. Through iterative 3D-printed versions, the gripper became more reliable across cables with outer diameters from 0.5 to 2.5 inches.

Safety Features and Outcome

Safety work included a normally closed emergency stop and ultrasonic sensors positioned to cover areas not visible to the LiDAR. These components were validated independently, creating a stronger base for future integration with the Raspberry Pi and the visual feedback system.

By the end of the semester, the project advanced the Boeing-sponsored robot in perception, safety, feedback, computation, and manipulation. The final design report documents the subsystem decisions, testing strategy, limitations, and next steps for future capstone teams to complete full system integration.

Our Engineering Process

Our engineering process was deeply rooted in structured concept development and iterative evaluation. We began by defining high-priority customer needs—such as reliable cable gripping, accurate obstacle avoidance, and clear visual feedback—and translated them into measurable technical requirements. To make informed design decisions, we utilized concept selection matrices to weigh various alternatives against criteria like cost, ease of implementation, and performance. For instance, when upgrading our main processor, we evaluated a Jetson Nano and a Raspberry Pi cluster before ultimately selecting the Raspberry Pi 5 for its optimal balance of compute power and software compatibility.

To efficiently tackle these challenges, we divided our development into several core sub-assemblies:

  • System & Navigation Upgrades: Jay upgraded the robot's "brain" to a Raspberry Pi 5, utilizing Docker to maintain compatibility with our ROS2 Humble architecture. For navigation, I implemented an A* reactive pathfinding algorithm utilizing LiDAR data to autonomously avoid obstacles. Talon also addressed the robot's tendency to drift on inclines by integrating a CMPS12 electronic compass to provide real-time corrective heading data.
  • Cable Detection & Manipulation: Darwin engineered a real-time cable detection pipeline using an Intel RealSense RGB-D camera. This system drew a green visual overlay over detected cables and computed their precise 3D (XYZ) coordinates for downstream path planning. To securely handle the cables, Jeremia completely redesigned the end-effector, 3D printing a fractal-vise-inspired gripper out of PLA that conformed to cables ranging from 0.5 to 2.5 inches in diameter.
  • Safety & Visual Feedback: Brody wrapped the robot's base in a WS2812 LED strip to provide immediate, color-coded visual feedback regarding the robot's operational state (e.g., autonomous mode, tele-op, or e-stop). For safety, Anderson installed a hardware-level Emergency Stop button that immediately cut power to the wheel motors, supplemented by HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensors to detect obstacles within the LiDAR's 6-inch blind spot.

Project Management

We managed our project timeline through a rigorous, multi-layered testing strategy that emphasized modular development. Rather than waiting for full system integration, we evaluated each subsystem independently to ensure we stayed on schedule. For example, our visual feedback system was validated using standalone 6-bit string terminal inputs, and our safety sensors were tested on a separate movable cart. Similarly, the cable detection software progressed through offline functionality checks, node-level testing using recorded data playback, and finally, hardware-in-the-loop physical validation. This phased approach allowed us to isolate bugs early, adapt to hardware delays, and successfully deliver a more robust, deployable robotic assistant by the end of the semester.

Spring 2026 Software Design & Documentation

Grocery Finder

Team: Vibe Coders

Co-authors: Alan Lee, Qizheng Sui, Sebastian Grammas

GitHub: SDD_Grocery_Pathfinding

Grocery Finder is a full-stack grocery pathfinding application that transforms a shopping list into an optimized store route. The app supports store selection, grocery item matching, aisle-based route planning, carts, coupons, price-aware shopping decisions, and user preferences such as store-brand and accessibility-aware planning.

Agile Scrum Process

The project was developed through an agile Scrum framework, with work organized into sprint increments rather than a single final build. Each sprint emphasized planning, implementation, review, and adjustment, so the team could respond to feedback while keeping a usable product increment moving forward.

  • Sprint planning: The team broke the product into scoped goals such as authentication, store selection, item search, route optimization, coupon integration, and release packaging.
  • Product backlog refinement: Features were prioritized around user value, implementation risk, and dependencies between frontend, backend, database, and documentation work.
  • Iterative releases: Interim, beta, and final release deliverables documented the application's growth from core grocery-list routing into a more complete shopping assistant.
  • Sprint review and documentation: Sprint decks and release deliverables captured progress, blockers, design decisions, testing evidence, and next steps for the following iteration.
  • Team coordination: The Scrum cadence helped the team divide work across UI development, Flask API routes, database models, optimization services, and formal SD&D documentation.

Technical Implementation

The frontend was built as a React and Vite application with step-based user flows for account access, store selection, grocery list entry, and optimized route review. The backend uses Flask with SQLAlchemy models for users, stores, grocery items, carts, prices, coupons, and store availability.

The route optimization system uses a Factory Method structure to select matching and planning strategies. Product matchers handle default matching, store-brand preference, budget-aware behavior, and coupon-aware behavior, while aisle planners support default and accessibility-friendly route planning. This structure kept the code extensible as Scrum iterations added new user-facing options.

Release Outcomes

By the final release, the project connected the major pieces expected of a Software Design & Documentation course project: a usable full-stack application, a documented architecture, iterative Scrum artifacts, release deliverables, and presentation material. The result was not just a prototype, but a documented software product that reflected multiple cycles of planning, implementation, testing, and review.
